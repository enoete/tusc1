Hook — Grab their attention
The hook is the very first thing you say. It should make the audience stop, think, and lean forward. A great hook asks a question, paints a picture, or says something surprising. You have about 10 seconds to win them over.
The question hook
"Raise your hand if you've ever been sitting right next to someone — maybe a friend, a parent, even a brother or sister — and they didn't even notice you were there. Because they were on their phone."
Why it works: hands go up, people laugh or feel seen, and you've already made your point before you've argued anything. The audience proves it for you.
The scene-setting hook
"Picture a family having dinner together. The food is hot. The table is set. But nobody is talking. Mum is scrolling. Dad is checking emails. The kids are watching videos. They are in the same room — but they are completely alone."
Why it works: the audience can see it clearly in their minds. It's specific. It might even be their own family.
The personal hook (use your own story)
"I wrote a song called Screen Time. Not because someone told me to. But because I looked around and realised that the screens in our lives were slowly replacing the people in our lives. And I was tired of it."
Why it works: it's real, it's you, and it immediately tells the audience why they should listen to you specifically.
The surprising fact hook
"Did you know that the average person checks their phone over 150 times a day? That's once every ten minutes — including when they're with the people they love most."
Why it works: numbers shock people. It makes them think about their own habits before you've even made your argument.
Define — Explain what isolation really means
Most people think isolation means being completely alone with no one around. Your job is to redefine it. Isolation can happen even in a full room. This is the idea that makes your entire argument powerful — and different from what anyone expects.
The redefinition
"When most people hear the word isolated, they picture someone sitting alone in a dark room. But I want to talk about a different kind of isolation. The kind where you are surrounded by people — and still feel completely invisible."
Why it works: it surprises them. It opens up a bigger, more interesting argument than they expected.
The presence vs. connection contrast
"Being present and being connected are two very different things. Your body can be in the room. But if your mind is on a screen, you are not connected to the people right in front of you. That gap between presence and connection — that is isolation."
Why it works: the contrast is clean and memorable. "Presence vs. connection" is a phrase they'll carry with them after the presentation.
The simple, direct definition
"Isolation means feeling cut off — cut off from real conversations, cut off from the people who care about you, cut off from the world happening right outside your door. Technology hasn't made us less connected online. But it has made us more cut off from the people standing right beside us."
Why it works: short, clear, and it lands the core argument before the evidence even begins.
Evidence — Build your case
Evidence is where you prove your point. Use a mix: personal stories, observations, facts, and examples from your own life and song. You don't need 20 pieces of evidence. Three or four strong ones beat ten weak ones every time.
Family evidence — from your song
"In my song Screen Time, I sing about the simple things children are missing — playing outside, bedtime stories, running around with friends. These aren't big things. But they are the things that build a family. And they're disappearing — one scroll at a time."
Why it works: it connects directly to something you've already done, making you credible and passionate at the same time.
Friendship evidence
"Think about how friendships have changed. We send heart emojis instead of giving real hugs. We post birthday wishes instead of showing up. We have hundreds of followers — and sometimes, not one real friend to call when something goes wrong."
Why it works: almost everyone in the audience has felt this, even if they've never said it out loud.
Caribbean community evidence — your local angle
"Here in St. Kitts, we grew up in a culture where your neighbour was your family. Where children played in the streets until the sun went down. Where people sat on their galleries and talked. That culture is being replaced — by Wi-Fi, by data plans, by screens that keep us indoors and keep us quiet."
Why it works: it's local, it's emotional, and nobody else arguing this topic has this exact angle. This is yours.
Mental health evidence
"Studies have found that teenagers who spend more time on social media are more likely to feel lonely, anxious, and left out — even though they're technically more 'connected' than any generation before them. More followers. More likes. More loneliness."
Why it works: the final three-word rhythm — "more followers, more likes, more loneliness" — is striking and easy to remember.
Rebut — Tackle the other side
A strong presenter doesn't ignore people who disagree — they address them and then push back. This shows maturity and makes your argument harder to knock down. The pattern is simple: "I hear you. You're right that... But here's why that's still not enough."
The "yes, but" rebut
"Now, I know what some of you might be thinking. Technology connects us too — we can video call relatives in another country, we can message friends in seconds. And you're right. Technology can connect us. But can a video call replace sitting in the same room as someone you love? Can a text message replace a real conversation? I don't think so."
Why it works: you give the other side credit, which makes you seem fair — and then you take it back with a question they can't argue with.
The quality vs. quantity rebut
"Yes, technology gives us more connections than ever before. But connection isn't just about quantity — how many people you know. It's about quality — how deeply you know them. And a screen will never give you the depth of a real, face-to-face relationship."
Why it works: the quantity/quality contrast is clean, memorable, and very hard to argue against.
The wrong-tool rebut
"Technology is wonderful for staying in touch across distances. But most of us aren't using it to call our grandparents overseas. We're using it to scroll past people we already see every day. We're using a long-distance tool — on people who are right beside us."
Why it works: it's a little funny, a little painful, and completely true. That combination is powerful.
Close — Leave them with something
The close is what they remember when they walk out of the room. It should feel like a conclusion but also like a challenge or an invitation. End with energy — not a trail-off. You want them to feel something, and you want them to want to do something.
The challenge close
"I have one challenge for you. Just one. Today — not next week, not someday — today, put down your phone for one hour. Sit with your family. Go outside. Talk to someone face to face. And see what happens. I promise you won't regret it."
Why it works: it's specific, it's doable, and it gives the audience something concrete to actually do. Words become action.
The callback close — ties back to your hook
"Remember that family I described at the beginning — sitting at a table, together but alone? I don't want that to be your family. I don't want that to be mine. We have a choice. And the choice starts with simply looking up."
Why it works: callbacks feel satisfying. It shows you planned the whole presentation. And "simply looking up" is quiet, poetic, and lands beautifully.
The personal close — your crown, your proof
"I am Adiaha. I am the Junior Calypso Monarch. And I didn't win that crown by scrolling through a screen. I won it by picking up a pen, writing something I believed in, and standing on a stage and saying it out loud. That is what happens when we put down the phones and pick up our lives. Thank you."
Why it works: it's powerful, personal, and it makes the crown part of the argument. Not just a title — living proof of what you're saying.
The quiet, emotional close
"The people who love you most are not on the other side of a screen. They are right here — in your house, in your classroom, on your street. Don't make them wait for you to notice them. Look up. Be here. While you still can."
Why it works: "while you still can" adds unexpected emotional weight. It's the kind of close that makes a room go quiet.

The goal

The goal is NOT to memorise word-for-word. That makes you sound robotic and panics you if you forget one sentence. The goal is to memorise the structure and a few key phrases, then speak naturally from what you know.

The 5-Part Skeleton

Step 1  Learn these five words first

Your whole presentation lives inside five words. Write them on your hand if you need to:

  1. Hook — grab their attention with a question or scene
  2. Define — explain what isolation really means (presence ≠ connection)
  3. Evidence — family, friends, community, facts, your personal story
  4. Rebut — agree technology connects, then explain why it’s still not enough
  5. Close — a challenge or call to action that sticks
Memory Techniques

Step 2  The hand technique

Hold up your hand. Each finger = one section of your talk. When you practise, physically touch each finger as you move through the sections. If you get nervous on stage, glance at your hand — it tells you exactly where you are and what comes next. Your body becomes your notes.

Step 3  One vivid image per section

Pick one picture in your mind for each section. Your brain remembers pictures better than words.

  • Hook: a family at dinner, food getting cold, everyone on their phones
  • Define: a person in a crowded room, completely invisible and alone
  • Evidence: your Screen Time song playing — let the lyrics remind you of the examples
  • Rebut: a video call vs. a real hug — feel the difference
  • Close: someone putting down their phone and picking up a friend’s hand
Practice Plan

Step 4  Practise in three stages

  1. Day 1–2 (Read aloud): Read your written answers out loud three times each day. Do not try to memorise yet. Just get comfortable with how the ideas sound.
  2. Day 3–4 (Cover and try): Close the document. Try to say each section using only the five-word skeleton. It will be messy. That is perfectly fine — this is how memory forms.
  3. Day 5+ (Full run-throughs): Deliver the complete presentation to a mirror, to dad, or record yourself. Time yourself — aim for 4 to 6 minutes. Repeat until it feels natural, not perfect.
On the Day

Step 5  Handle nerves like a Calypso Queen

  • Take one slow, deep breath before you start. It signals your brain to calm down.
  • Speak slower than you think you need to — nerves make us rush.
  • If you forget something, pause, smile, and move to the next section. The audience will not know.
  • You have already performed on stage and won a crown. This is just a conversation with people who already believe in you.

Your personal edge

Most people arguing this topic are just sharing facts. You have something they do not — you wrote a song about it, you performed it on a stage, you won with it, and you have visited schools to spread the message. Always remind your audience:

“I don’t just believe this — I have been living this message.”
